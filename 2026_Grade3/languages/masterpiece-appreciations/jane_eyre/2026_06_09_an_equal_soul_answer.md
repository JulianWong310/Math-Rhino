# Excerpt from *Jane Eyre* — Answer Key & Detailed Explanations

### Answer Key & Explanations

#### Part 1: Multiple-Choice Answers
* **Question 1: B** *Explanation:* "Custom" and "conventionalities" represent the rigid social structure of Victorian England, which viewed a poor governess as inferior to her master. By rejecting these mediums, Jane bypasses social hierarchy to declare direct, spiritual equality.
* **Question 2: B** *Explanation:* To "ensnare" means to catch in a snare or trap. Jane uses it to show that her independent will cannot be captured or restrained.
* **Question 3: C** *Explanation:* The "net" represents societal containment and dependencies. By declaring she is "no bird," Jane rejects the role of a captive, powerless creature kept for a master's pleasure, asserting her complete personal autonomy.
* **Question 4: C** *Explanation:* Jane explicitly emphasizes that laws and principles are not meant for easy times, but precisely for "such moments as this, when body and soul rise in mutiny against their rigour"—meaning when passion and severe temptation threaten to overthrow moral judgment.
* **Question 5: C** *Explanation:* The idiomatic expression "to plant one's foot"  symbolizes her final, unyielding decision to stand firm on her values and refuse to compromise, no matter how intense the emotional pressure.

#### Part 2: Open-Response Exemplar Answers 

**6. In paragraph 1, Jane describes herself as "poor, obscure, plain, and little." Explain how she uses her concept of the "spirit" to overcome these social disadvantages. Use specific details from the text to support your answer.**
* **Exemplar Answer:** Jane uses the concept of the "spirit" to argue that human worth is independent of social standing, wealth, or physical appearance. Although she acknowledges her worldly disadvantages of being "poor, obscure, plain, and little," she states that these factors do not define her humanity, declaring, "I have as much soul as you, — and full as much heart!" She elevates the conversation beyond earthly limits by stating, "it is my spirit that addresses your spirit." By concluding that before God they would stand "passed through the grave" as equals, Jane establishes that her inner spiritual identity completely bypasses and overcomes the artificial barriers of Victorian social class.

**7. In paragraph 3, Jane states, "The more solitary, the more friendless, the more unsustained I am, the more I will respect myself." Explain how this statement reflects her inner strength. Support your response with ideas from the passage.**
* **Exemplar Answer:** This statement reflects Jane’s profound inner strength because it demonstrates that her self-worth comes from within, rather than from external validation or comfort. Usually, being "solitary," "friendless," and "unsustained" makes a person vulnerable to giving in to temptation for the sake of companionship. However, Jane declares that the harsher her loneliness becomes, the more she will "respect myself" and "keep the law given by God." Even when her "body and soul rise in mutiny" and her emotional passion feels like "veins running fire," her inner strength allows her to rely on her "preconceived opinions" and "foregone determinations." By choosing isolation over a compromised moral standard, Jane reveals an unyielding character that protects its own integrity at all costs.

# The Emperor's New Clothes
## by Hans Christian Andersen (English translation by H.P. Paull)

Many years ago, there was an Emperor, who was so excessively fond of new clothes, that he spent all his money in dress. He did not trouble himself in the least about his soldiers; nor did he care to go either to the theatre or the chase, except for the opportunities then afforded him for displaying his new clothes. He had a different suit for each hour of the day; and as of any other king or emperor, one is accustomed to say, "he is sitting in council," it was always said of him, "The Emperor is sitting in his wardrobe."

In the great city where he lived, life was always gay; every day many strangers came there. One day two swindlers came to this city; they gave out that they were weavers, and said they could weave the most beautiful cloths imaginable. Not only were the colors and patterns unusually fine, but the clothes made of these cloths possessed the wonderful property of being invisible to any one who was either unfit for the office he held, or was very stupid.

"Those would indeed be capital clothes," thought the Emperor, "if I had them, I should be able to find out which men in my empire are not fit for the places they have; I could tell the clever from the stupid. Yes, this stuff must be woven for me directly!" And he gave the two swindlers a large sum of money, that they might begin their work without delay.

They set up two looms, and pretended to be working, but they had nothing at all on the looms. They demanded the finest silk and the best gold, which they put into their own pockets, and worked at the empty looms far into the night.

"I should very much like to know how they are getting on with the cloth," thought the Emperor. But he felt a little uncomfortable when he remembered that foolish people were unable to see the cloth. Certainly he thought he had nothing to fear for himself, but still he thought it would be better to send somebody else first, to see how the work was going on. Everybody in the town knew of the wonderful power of the cloth, and were all anxious to see how bad or how stupid their neighbors were.

"I will send my honest old minister to the weavers," thought the Emperor. "He can judge best how the cloth looks, for he has sense, and no one can be more worthy of his office than he is."

So the good old minister went into the hall where the two swindlers were working at the empty looms. "Heaven preserve me!" thought the old minister, opening his eyes wide, "I cannot see anything at all!" But he did not say so.

Both the swindlers begged him to be so kind as to come nearer, and asked if he did not approve of the colors and the pattern. Then they pointed to the empty looms, and the poor old minister stared as hard as he could, but he could see nothing, for there was nothing to see.

"Dear me!" thought he, "can I indeed be so stupid? I should never have thought it, and nobody must know it! Is it possible that I am not fit for my office? No, no, I cannot say that I was unable to see the cloth."

"Now, have you nothing to say?" said one of the swindlers, while he pretended to be busily weaving.

"Oh, it is charming — quite delightful!" answered the old minister, looking through his spectacles. "What a fine pattern, and what beautiful colors! I shall tell the Emperor that I like the cloth very much."

"We are very glad to hear you say so," said both the swindlers, and then they named all the colors and explained the strange pattern. The old minister listened attentively, that he might be able to repeat it to the Emperor; and so he did.

The swindlers now demanded more money, more silk, and more gold, for weaving, but they put it all into their own pockets; not a single thread was put into the cloth, but they continued to work at the empty looms as before.

The Emperor soon sent another worthy statesman to see how the weaving was going on, and if the cloth would soon be ready. The same thing happened to him as to the minister; he looked and looked, but because there was nothing on the empty looms, he could see nothing.

"Is it not a beautiful piece of cloth?" asked the two swindlers, showing and explaining the fine pattern and colors which were not there.

"I know I am not stupid," thought the man, "so it must be that I am not worthy of my good office. That is very strange, but I must not let any one know it." And he praised the cloth which he did not see, and told them how much he was delighted with the beautiful colors and the exquisite pattern. "It is perfectly charming," he said to the Emperor.

All the people in the town were talking about the precious cloth. At last the Emperor wished to see it himself, while it was still on the loom. So, with a number of chosen courtiers, among whom were the two honest statesmen who had already seen the cloth, he went to the two swindlers.

"Is it not magnificent?" said the two statesmen. "See, your Majesty, what a pattern! what colors!" And they pointed to the empty loom, for they believed that all others could see the cloth.

"What!" thought the Emperor, "I see nothing at all! This is terrible! Am I stupid? Am I not fit to be Emperor? That would indeed be the most dreadful thing that could happen to me."

"Oh, it is very beautiful!" said the Emperor. "It has my highest approval." And he nodded pleasantly, and gazed at the empty loom, for he would not say that he could see nothing. All his attendants stared and stared, but saw nothing more than the others; nevertheless, they all exclaimed, "Oh, how beautiful!" And they advised him to wear these splendid new clothes for the first time at the great procession that was soon to take place. "Magnificent! charming! excellent!" was heard on all sides, and every one was in the best humor. The Emperor gave each of the swindlers a title and a medal for their cleverness, and they called themselves "Knights of the Order of the Weavers."

The swindlers sat up the whole night before the day of the procession, and burnt more than sixteen candles. They pretended to take the cloth from the loom, cut it with scissors in the air, and sewed with needles without thread. At last they said, "The Emperor's new clothes are ready!"

The Emperor, with all his courtiers, then came to the hall. The swindlers held up their arms as if they were holding something, and said, "See! here are the trousers! here is the coat! here is the mantle!" and so on. "They are as light as a spider's web, so that one would think he had nothing on; but that is the beauty of them."

"Yes," said all the courtiers, but they could see nothing, for there was nothing to see.

"Will your Majesty please to condescend to take off your clothes?" said the swindlers, "so that we may put on you the new clothes, here before the mirror."

The Emperor took off all his clothes, and the swindlers pretended to put on him the new clothes, piece by piece; they took him round the waist and seemed to be fastening something — it was the train; and the Emperor turned round and round before the mirror.

"How beautifully they fit! how well they sit!" said every one. "What a pattern! what colors! It is indeed a magnificent suit of clothes!"

"The canopy that is to be carried over your Majesty in the procession is waiting," said the Master of the Ceremonies.

"Well, I am quite ready," said the Emperor. "Does not my suit fit me beautifully?" and he turned himself round again before the mirror, as if he were examining his handsome figure.

The chamberlains, who were to carry the train, stretched out their hands as if to hold it; they pretended to lift something from the ground, and said, "Yes, the train is quite right."

So the Emperor walked on under the beautiful canopy, and all the people in the streets and at the windows said, "Oh, how beautiful the Emperor's new clothes are! What a magnificent train! What a perfect fit!" No one would let it be supposed that he could not see anything, for that would prove him either very stupid or unfit for his office. No clothes of the Emperor had ever been such a success.

"But he has nothing on!" said a little child.

"Listen to the innocent!" said the father; and one whispered to another what the child had said.

"But he has nothing on!" a child says! has nothing on!

At last the people caught hold of the child's words and repeated them aloud. "But he has nothing on!" cried all the people. It was indeed a great joy to the Emperor that he had nothing on, for now he knew that he was not stupid, and that he was fit to be Emperor. So he held himself still higher, and the procession moved on, and the courtiers carried the train which did not exist.

## Excerpt from The Old Man and the Sea

### The Final Tug of War

**[1]** The sun was rising for the third time since he had gone out to sea when the fish started to circle. The old man, Santiago, could not see the fish yet, but he felt the sudden change in the tension of the heavy line. "It is a very big circle," he whispered to the empty ocean. "But he is circling. Soon our true work begins." He braced his aching back against the wooden seat of his small skiff, shifting his weight carefully so that he could put his left hand into the salt water. His hands were stiff and severely cut from holding the heavy line for two days, and they had begun to resemble dried, twisted vines. "Pain does not matter to a man," he thought, though his swollen muscles throbbed with a relentless ache.

**[2]** For two hours, the giant fish moved in its slow, majestic circle beneath the dark water. It was a powerful phantom, hidden from sight but demanding every drop of the old man’s remaining strength. With each turn, Santiago had to pull the line in inch by inch, fighting for every single foot of rope. Twice, he felt faint and dizzy, and black spots danced before his eyes. He lifted some sea water with his uninjured hand and rubbed it on his face and the back of his neck to keep from passing out. "I cannot fail and die on a fish like this," he prayed. "God help me endure. You have to last. Don’t even think of quitting."

**[3]** It was on the third turn that Santiago saw the fish for the first time. He saw it first as a dark, elongated shadow that took so long to pass completely under the boat that he could not believe its length. "No," he gasped, his breath ragged in his chest. "He cannot be that big." But the fish was that big. At the end of the next circle, the giant marlin broke the surface only thirty yards away. The sun reflected brightly off its silver belly, and its purple stripes showed clearly in the morning light. Its sword-like bill was as long as a baseball bat, and it rose out of the water like a great phantom, brilliantly and beautifully alive, before sliding back into the deep blue sea. 

**[4]** The sight of the magnificent creature filled the old man with a strange mixture of awe and sorrow. His body was nearly destroyed, his hands were raw like butchered meat, yet his inner determination remained unshaken. He looked at the deep water where the shadow disappeared and whispered, "You are killing me, fish. But you have a right to. I have never seen or heard of anyone more beautiful or calm or noble than you, brother. Come on and kill me. I do not care who kills who." Santiago wiped the sweat from his eyes and gripped the line once more with a grim determination. "A man is not made for defeat," he reminded himself. "A man can be destroyed but not defeated."

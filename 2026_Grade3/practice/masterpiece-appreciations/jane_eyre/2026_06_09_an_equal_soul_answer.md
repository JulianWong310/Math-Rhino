# Excerpt from *Jane Eyre* — Answer Key & Detailed Explanations

### Part 1: Multiple-Choice Questions — Answer Key & Explanations

**1. In paragraph 1, what does the word "automaton" suggest about how Jane refuses to be treated?**
* **Correct Answer:** **B) As a mechanical object that has no independent thoughts or feelings.**
* **Explanation:** An "automaton" is a moving mechanical device or robot. By asking *"Do you think I am an automaton?—a machine without feelings?"*, Jane is fiercely rejecting the idea that she can be treated as an unfeeling object that simply obeys orders without emotional responses.

**2. Which phrase from paragraph 2 best demonstrates Jane’s belief in absolute equality?**
* **Correct Answer:** **B) "we stood at God’s feet, equal,—as we are!"**
* **Explanation:** This phrase directly states her core message. Jane is arguing that when you strip away earthly differences like wealth, gender, and social status, human spirits are inherently equal (*"it is my spirit that addresses your spirit... equal,—as we are!"*).

**3. According to paragraph 3, how does Mr. Rochester react to Jane's speech?**
* **Correct Answer:** **B) He looks at her with a mixture of wonder and respect, and his grim expression softens.**
* **Explanation:** This is a direct retrieval question. Paragraph 3 explicitly states that Mr. Rochester *"stared at her in complete awe, his grim expression softening as he looked into her fierce, flashing eyes."* **4. What is the main theme of Jane’s speech in this passage?**
* **Correct Answer:** **C) The fundamental equality of human souls, regardless of wealth, gender, or social class.**
* **Explanation:** The entire passage centers on Jane demanding respect and asserting that her poverty (*"poor, obscure, plain, and little"*) does not mean she lacks a soul or feelings. It is a powerful declaration of spiritual and human equality, which is why it is considered a classic feminist speech.

### Part 2: Open-Response Questions — Exemplar Answers

**5. Based on paragraph 1, explain how Jane uses contrasting ideas to argue against the unfair assumptions made about her. Use specific details from the text to support your answer.**

#### **Sample Answer **
In paragraph 1, Jane uses strong contrasts between physical/social limitations and spiritual fullness to argue that she deserves equal respect. She contrasts her external situation—being "poor, obscure, plain, and little"—with her inner self, declaring, "I have as much soul as you,—and full as much heart!" Furthermore, she contrasts her actual life with an imaginary one, stating that if God had given her "some beauty and much wealth," she would have made it just as difficult for Rochester to leave her. By placing these contrasting descriptions of low social status against her immense emotional and spiritual capacity, Jane successfully argues that human worth cannot be measured by appearance or money.

**6. Describe how the author creates a sense of intensity and determination during this encounter. Use ideas from the passage to support your response.**

#### **Sample Answer **
The author creates a sense of intensity and determination through powerful verbs, physical descriptions of resilience, and dramatic natural elements. First, Jane’s physical actions show defiance; she "shook herself loose" and "faced him directly," refusing to back down. Second, the text highlights her internal strength despite environmental discomfort, noting that she "braced herself against the cold evening air" with a voice full of "unyielding determination" and "stood her ground." Finally, the author uses the setting to match the emotional intensity, describing how the "wind rumbled through the old trees" while Jane spoke with "relentless power." These combined details make the confrontation feel incredibly fierce and serious.

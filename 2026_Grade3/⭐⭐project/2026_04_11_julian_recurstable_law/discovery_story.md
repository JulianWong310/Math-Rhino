# 发现的故事 | The Discovery Story
> **母亲的记录 (A Mother's Record)**

---

### 中文版 (Chinese Version)

周五下午，孩子在房间玩耍时，突然跟我说他有了一个新的数学发现。类似的“发现”我常听他提起——记得上一次他发现了 8 的整除规律，甚至知道百位数需要分奇偶来讨论。
这次他告诉我，5 的幂（次方）总会得到以 25 结尾的数字。我正准备拿计算器验证，他却说：“妈妈，不用拿计算器。因为 4 乘以 25 等于 100，所以多出来的 25 总会留在末尾。”
我像往常一样夸赞他，但他接下来的话让我吃惊。他说，如果把 5 的幂去掉尾部的 25，剩余部分的关系是：“上一个数字乘以 5 再加 1，等于下一个数字”。
起初我并没听懂，他急切地举例解释。当我理解其中的代数逻辑后，我意识到这是一个了不起的发现。他兴奋地问我能不能用他的名字命名，我想万一这个理论是数论中尚未被充分关注的一个分支，如果他未来想深入研究，现在留下的记录将有迹可循。于是我们便开始了一系列证明与命名。在此过程中，他灵感再次迸发，指出所有以 0、1、5、6 结尾的数字都具有类似的性质。他解释道：因为这些数字乘以自己后，末位依然是它本身，所以它们的幂在尾部一定是稳定的。基于此，我们对这部分新发现也进行了证明和命名。
考虑到他年龄尚小，不宜长时间使用电脑，大多时候是由我倾听他的口述并代为打字。我尝试用自己掌握的知识，尽可能用专业的学术词汇去“翻译”他的想法。历时两天，Julian 完成了他的第一份数学论文。
我观察发现，Julian 喜欢在脑海中演化数学过程，而不习惯动笔。问及灵感来源，他说这些规律都是突然“乍现”并被捕捉到的，并非刻意思考，而是自然而然地产生。他展现出的数学直觉让我深感责任重大，唯有加倍努力，才能更好地引导他的成长。

### English Version
On a Friday afternoon, while playing in my room, Julian suddenly told me he had made a new mathematical discovery. I often hear about such "discoveries" from him—I remember last time he figured out the divisibility rule for 8, even knowing that the hundreds digit must be discussed in terms of being parity (odd or even).
This time, he told me that powers of 5 always result in a number ending in 25. As I was about to reach for a calculator to verify, he said, "Mom, no need for a calculator. It’s obvious. Since 4 \times 25 = 100, the 'extra' 25 will always stay at the end."
I praised him as usual, but his next comment caught me off guard. He said that if you remove the "25" tail from the powers of 5, the relationship between the remaining parts is: "The previous number times 5, plus 1, equals the next number."
I didn't understand at first, so he eagerly explained with examples. Once I grasped the algebraic logic behind it, I realized this was a remarkable discovery. He excitedly asked if he could name it after himself. Thinking that this might be a niche branch of number theory that hasn't been fully explored, and wanting to ensure he has a trail to follow if he pursues deeper research in the future, we began a series of proofs and naming. During this process, his inspiration flared again—he pointed out that all numbers ending in 0, 1, 5, or 6 share similar properties. He explained: because these numbers result in themselves when multiplied, their "tails" must remain stable across their powers. Based on this, we formalized the proof and naming for this new expansion as well.
Given his young age, it isn't suitable for him to spend long hours on the computer. Most of the time, I listened to his dictation and typed for him. I tried to use my own mathematical knowledge to "translate" his ideas into professional academic terminology as much as possible. Over the course of two days, Julian completed his very first math paper.
I’ve observed that Julian loves to evolve mathematical processes entirely in his mind rather than writing them down. When asked about his inspiration, he said these patterns just "flash" into his mind, and he simply catches them. It isn't a result of forced thinking, but something that happens naturally. The mathematical intuition he displays makes me feel a profound sense of responsibility. I know I must work twice as hard to better guide his growth.
